# Impostor Syndrome: Why Competent People Feel Like Frauds

*By Daniela Coelho · Behavioral Research & Psychology*

---

Pauline Clance and Suzanne Imes coined the term in 1978. Studying high-achieving academic women, they found something unexpected: despite objective evidence of competence — degrees, publications, peer recognition — these women secretly believed they had fooled everyone. That they were frauds. That any moment, someone would discover the truth.

Clance and Imes called it the "impostor phenomenon." Today we know it isn't exclusive to women, or to academic high-achievers. It's transversal to gender, culture, and field. And it's probably affecting someone you know — or you — right now.

## The Paradox of Perceived Incompetence

Bertrand Russell's observation resonates precisely here: "The whole problem with the world is that fools and fanatics are always so certain of themselves, and wiser people so full of doubts."

Psychology confirms this systematically. The Dunning-Kruger effect — published in 1999 in the Journal of Personality and Social Psychology — demonstrated that people with low competence in an area consistently overestimate their abilities. They can't evaluate what they don't know.

The inverse is equally true: experts consistently underestimate the difficulty of tasks they execute fluently, assuming others could do the same. The more you know, the more aware you are of what you don't know.

Impostor syndrome is, in part, a byproduct of metacognition. It's the price of self-awareness.

## Five Patterns Clance Identified

**The impostor cycle.** Task → fear of failure → overwork or procrastination → success → attribution to luck or extreme effort → no internalization of success → new task, same fear.

**External attribution.** When things go well: luck, timing, other people's generosity. When things go badly: personal incompetence. Success is never merit. Failure always is.

**Fear of evaluation.** Not just fear of failing, but of being *discovered*. The job interview isn't about demonstrating competence — it's about not revealing the lack of it.

**Minimization of achievement.** "Anyone would have done the same." "It wasn't anything special." "I just got lucky with the circumstances."

**Performance anxiety.** The belief that any mistake will confirm others' suspicion — even when no such suspicion exists — that you're less capable than you appear.

## What Happens in the Brain

The amygdala doesn't distinguish well between physical and social threats. For the brain, being "found out" as incompetent activates the same alarm systems as a survival threat — because for social primates, exclusion from the group was fatal.

This explains the disproportionate emotional intensity. It's not dramatic exaggeration. It's evolutionary biology operating in a new context.

## Why More Success Doesn't Solve It

The trap: logically, more evidence of competence should reduce the feeling of impostorhood. In practice, it often increases it.

Each promotion raises the standard. Each achievement creates a new audience to potentially disappoint. The range of situations where "being discovered" could happen expands.

Valerie Young, who has spent decades studying the phenomenon, notes that impostor syndrome doesn't resolve through credential accumulation. It resolves through a fundamental shift in the relationship with competence and error.

## What Actually Helps

**Normalize incompetence in new domains.** Most people with impostor syndrome apply expert-level standards to learning situations. Making mistakes while learning isn't evidence that you're a fraud — it's evidence that you're learning.

**Separate feeling from fact.** "I feel like a fraud" and "I am a fraud" are different propositions. One is internal experience. The other is a claim about reality. Impostor syndrome conflates them.

**Build an evidence file.** Literally. A list of accomplishments, problems solved, positive feedback received. Not to convince yourself you're perfect, but to have access to concrete data when feeling overrides fact.

**Talk to others.** Secrecy feeds the syndrome. Discovering that admired colleagues experience the same thing is often the beginning of release.

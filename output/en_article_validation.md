# Validation Addiction: Why We Need Approval All the Time

*By Daniela Coelho · Behavioral Research & Psychology*

---

You posted something and kept checking the likes for the first five minutes. You felt a small but real sense of relief when someone commented something positive. When engagement stopped, something felt off.

This isn't vanity. It's neurobiology.

## The Chemistry of Approval

David Rock at the NeuroLeadership Institute developed the SCARF model mapping what the social brain monitors constantly: Status, Certainty, Autonomy, Relatedness, Fairness.

Threats to status — including perceived disapproval or indifference — activate the same brain regions as physical pain. This isn't metaphor. It's neuroimaging.

For our ancestors, group approval meant inclusion. And inclusion meant survival. Social rejection was not merely uncomfortable — it was dangerous. The brain that evolved in that environment doesn't distinguish well between rejection on social media and rejection from the tribe. Both trigger the alarm.

## Why Variable Reward Is Particularly Addictive

B.F. Skinner discovered in the 1950s the most effective reinforcement schedule for maintaining behavior: variable ratio reinforcement. Not the guaranteed reward — but the reward that sometimes comes, sometimes doesn't, unpredictably.

Pigeons in chambers with this schedule pressed levers compulsively. They couldn't stop.

Social platforms apply exactly this schedule. You don't know when you'll get a like, a comment, a message. So you check. And check again. The dopamine released by each check isn't from the reward itself — it's from the anticipation of the reward.

Adam Alter at NYU documented this extensively: it's the same mechanism that explains gambling addiction.

## Five Signs of Validation Dependence

Difficulty making decisions without consulting others — not for lack of opinion, but fear that your own opinion might be wrong, and that perception of wrongness would be judged.

Mood highly dependent on external feedback. A critical comment destroys the day. A compliment saves it. Emotional state is outsourced.

Difficulty disagreeing — not because you agree, but because disagreeing creates the risk of disapproval.

Overperformance to be seen. Working beyond what's necessary, beyond your own limits, to ensure recognition. The effort comes not from the task but from what the task can signal about you.

Obsessive analysis of how you were perceived. Replaying conversations, posts, presentations for signs of approval or rejection. The mental loop that doesn't stop.

## What Actually Changes Things

Build self-esteem based on values, not performance. "I am someone who acts with integrity" doesn't oscillate with every external evaluation. "I am someone who does good work" oscillates every time someone disagrees.

Practice actions without audience deliberately. Do things nobody will know you did. Help someone anonymously. Create something you won't show. Strengthen the intrinsic motivation muscle.

Delay social checking cycles. Don't check metrics immediately after posting. Wait 24 hours. It reduces the dopaminergic variable reinforcement cycle.

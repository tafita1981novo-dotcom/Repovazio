# When Someone Makes You Doubt Your Own Reality: Understanding Gaslighting

*By Daniela Coelho · Behavioral Research & Psychology*

---

The term comes from a 1944 film. In Gaslight, a husband systematically manipulates his wife until she begins to doubt her own perception of reality.

Eighty years later, the concept became cultural shorthand. But such frequent use has cost it precision: not every disagreement is gaslighting. Not every difficult person is a gaslighter.

When it is real though — when reality distortion is systematic — the impact is devastating in ways psychology can specifically map.

## What Makes Gaslighting Effective

Jennifer Freyd at the University of Oregon coined betrayal trauma: when the source of harm is also the source of care, the cognitive system often blocks perception of the abuse.

It is a survival adaptation. Recognizing that someone you love or depend on is hurting you is cognitively costly. So the brain creates zones of not-knowing. You sense something is wrong but cannot name what.

This is exactly what gaslighting exploits.

## Five Patterns That Are Not Just Conflict

Reality denial of documented facts. Not about interpretations — about facts. Reframing emotion as pathology: your emotional response becomes the problem, not the behavior causing it. Systematic deflection: the original point is never resolved. Selective validation: their perceptions are always legitimate, yours always suspect. Progressive erosion of external reference points through isolation.

## What Helps

Document immediately after difficult situations before the narrative gets rewritten. Reconnect with trusted people outside the relationship. Name the pattern internally. Seek specialized support — therapy focused on relational trauma has the best evidence for rebuilding trust in your own perception.

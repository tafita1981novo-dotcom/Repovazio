# Emotional Boundaries: Protection vs. Isolation — What Research Shows

*By Daniela Coelho · Behavioral Research & Psychology*

---

"Set a boundary" has become universal advice. But a genuine emotional boundary — one that protects without isolating, that preserves connection while preserving you — is far more nuanced than the pop psychology version suggests.

## What Tawwab Found

Nedra Tawwab, therapist and researcher who spent years studying boundaries in relationships, offers the most useful definition: boundaries are expectations and needs that help you feel safe and comfortable in your relationships. They are communication, not punishment.

That distinction matters enormously. Most people learn about boundaries through the lens of conflict — what to do when someone crosses a line. Far fewer consider boundaries as ongoing communication about what you need to feel secure.

## The Gottman Distinction

John Gottman's research on couples identified two types of emotional withdrawal that look similar from the outside but function very differently.

**Defensive avoidance** preserves the self while keeping connection possible. It's stepping back from a heated conversation, asking for space before responding, taking time to regulate before continuing.

**Stonewalling** — complete withdrawal that ends the possibility of repair — is qualitatively different. It's not a boundary. It's a wall. And in Gottman's longitudinal research, stonewalling was one of the four strongest predictors of relationship dissolution.

Healthy boundaries are the first. Walls are the second. The terminology often conflates them.

## How to Tell the Difference

**Boundaries have language.** You can articulate what you need — "I need space when we're arguing, but I want to continue this conversation later." Walls communicate through silence, withdrawal, or cutting contact without explanation.

**Boundaries are flexible.** They adapt to the relationship and context. Walls are rigid regardless of who's on the other side or what happened.

**Boundaries preserve relationship.** The goal is to continue connecting in a healthier way. Walls end the possibility of connection.

**Boundaries come from values.** "I don't accept yelling because mutual respect is non-negotiable for me." Walls come from pain: "I cut off anyone who disappoints me because trusting hurts too much."

**Boundaries are communicated.** The other person knows what you need. Walls leave people guessing what they did wrong.

## The Developmental Root

Why do walls form? Often, they're protective responses to environments where expressing needs led to harm, abandonment, or ridicule. If asking for what you needed as a child resulted in rejection, withdrawal becomes adaptive. The nervous system learns: don't ask, don't show, don't need.

In adult relationships, this looks like emotional unavailability, frequent cutting of contact, or relationships that don't pass a certain depth before ending.

## What Changes Things

**Practice communicating needs before cutting.** The formula is simple, if uncomfortable: "When X happens, I feel Y. I need Z." That's a boundary. Disappearing without explanation is a wall.

**Distinguish safety from discomfort.** Boundaries protect genuine emotional safety. The discomfort of difficult conversations isn't the same as a boundary violation.

**Work on vulnerability tolerance.** Walls often protect against past pain, not present threat. That's territory for therapy — specifically for the attachment patterns that underlie them.

Real boundaries don't push people away. They draw the right people closer, in the right way, at the right depth.
